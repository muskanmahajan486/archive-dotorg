I am not aware of any other powerline based technologies except maybe "digitalstrom".

Z-Wave, Zigbee and KNX RF all have feedback. KNX RF is the most expensive version but offers a lot especially if you later want to extend and you would be able to run some wires. They offer gateways to link KNX RF to KNX TP. Zigbee has the problem that it is a standard but only on a lower level which means not all devices can communicate with each other.

Z-Wave already offers a lot devices and they can talk with each other. Mains powered devices act as a repeater to be able to communicate on bigger distances.

Another RF protocol coming up is ENOCEAN and somebody is already working on OpenRemote integrations.

For old-fashioned 220V lighting I'm not aware of any to recommend (short of something where you build everything from scratch yourself) mostly due to the liabilities involved with distribution of dangerous voltage, what you mentioned before.

So on Open Source lighting side the options come down to low-voltage and LED lighting.

Daniel's panStamp is one option there, checking the store there's an 10A/10V output actuator for example: http://www.panstamp.com/products/output-board

Another option is Akiba's Freaklabs which is Zigbee-based IIRC. There are some LED related items in the store, although in both cases will depend a bit on lighting requirements you have (ambient/background/spot) and whether you get enough out of these for your needs.

And to be clear in both cases you'll be well into the research/prototype mission, these are early and not proven yet.

I have been an avid X10 (advanced) user for some years now, and eventually took the plunge to get rid of it all for a number of reasons. 1). Reliability. 2). Its now obsolete.

So I looked around a lot at all of the Open & affordable systems like Z-Wave, Lutron, KNX, Helvar and so forth.

Eventually I stumbled on something new and currently (I think) only available in the UK. It's called LightwaveRF. It's not Powerline, it's RF, but it's mega reliable and really cheap (as an example the Switches come in a 3-pack with a Remote for just £35. The box that holds all of the sequences and timers and allows WAN access on an Android or iPhone is only £70.

What made this really favourite for me was when I contacted the nice people at LightwaveRF (a company called JSJS Designs) and asked them for the API, they sent it straight over. So now I have OpenRemote controlling my Lights and Appliances from my iPhone, Android Tablets and iPad and i have been able to build the mood control of the lights into my Home Theatre pages of my OpenRemote.

Unfortunately though I started a task to program OpenRemote to include the LightwaveRF Protocol so that instead of just sending UDP packets from OpenRemote, I would be able to properly integrate a bi-directional protocol with Sensors and Switches (etc).... but I have not had the time to progress this much yet

Anyway.... sorry for jumping in, just wanted to share my delights at this new technology (and no, I don;t work for them )

Hej Jonas,

thank you for writing down your thoughts, experiences, and questions. As you seem to be aware, there are several other systems essentially identical to "your" Nexa. I downloaded some "IR-codes" ("Nexia with switch code") from sydia.se and it turned out to be the well known codes used e.g. by Intertechno. Here in Germany, Intertechno are fairly common, "identical", at least in the sense of using identical codes, to products sold under names Düwi, One-for-all, ELRO, and probably more that I am not aware of. I am using a fairly large number of Intertechno switches myself, and the reliablity is OK. A slightly better, slightly more expensive system (but still "cheap") is FS20, using 868MHz frequency, thus less sensitive for disturbances from headless earphones and babyphones. It also has some smart dimming facilities: My good-nite macro turns my bedroom lights to 50%, and then dims it down to 0 over 5 minutes .

To control them, I use a T-10 from http://www.ezcontrol.de (now discontinued and replaced by the successor XS-1 model). This sends the RF signals (to Intertechno/Nexa/FS20,... ) directly from ethernet, using http interface, or UDP (just T-10, not XS-1). (I think there has been at least one thread about it.) Subjectively, this appears to work more reliable than the GlobalCache + IR->RF solution. As Swedish alternative to Ezcontrol is Telldus Tellstick, available both as USB version and for ethernet. (There has been some threads in the Eventghost forum.)

BTW, you should have a look at my program IrMaster, http://www.harctoolbox.org/irmaster.html, Among other things, you can generate all the "codewheel" signals for Intertechno/Nexa by selecting the "Intertechno" protocol, D for house code, S for device address, and F=1 for on, F=0 for off.
