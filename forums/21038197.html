If your time is limited, and you want to realise this in the next 6 months or so, imho OR on its own is not yet the single platform to realise this. If some money is not a limiting factor, I would consider something like Micasaverde as a solution to do the basic management and control for HVAC. An alternative might be the Fibaro HC. Both offer 24/7 dedicated control of your devices. Each has its own advantages. The Micasaverde seems to be the more open platform. I have seen some people on this forum discussing interaction with the Micasaverde with OpenRemote.

At the moment support for Z-Wave directly from OR is limited. Zigbee is not yet supported as far as I know. So Micasaverde could fill that gap if your choice were Z-Wave. I don't know if they already support the recently released locks. Do check their website
I think there is plenty of experience here of controlling audio/visual things with OpenRemote. The charm of OR is that it is in principle agnostic about what it controls. You can control devices directly or via a dedicated controller if that controller is sufficiently open.
Bear in mind that you should design your system such that it still can work if your automation controls are down.

Posted by dentedvw at Dec 23, 2012 19:01

As a three day old newbie, I've concluded that Open Remote (OR) is an incredibly powerful and elegant system...but the documentation is really awful. Don't get me wrong...it's all there, but figuring everything out is like playing an adventure game. Rather than just whine, I do have a suggestion that I think could improve things immeasurably; a comprehensive Table of Contents.

I've been using X10 modules for years, and decided to use a recently purchased Raspberry Pi as a dedicated controller for this purpose. I stumbled onto OR as a possible vehicle for this, and since I'm more conversant with Windows than Linux, opted to first install the controller on a laptop running Windows 7. Using my Android tablet, I just successfully rang the chime that indicates that my cat wants to come inside, and decided to share my thoughts with other potential newbies.

First...yes, X10 works just fine, and is very easy to set up.... once you've cracked the code. I have both the CM11A and the CM15A, but opted to use the CM11A because it seems simpler for programs to handle. I haven't bought a computer in years that had a serial port, but serial to USB adapters are really cheap, and Windows 7 has no problems findings drivers for them; plug it in, after it grinds for a while, it reports that it is successfully installed, and tells you what COMM port it looks like to the computer. It may not be this simple with Linux...but I'm sure I can figure it out. Enter this into the config.properties file, and that's it...the controller works fine with it. Where is the config.properties file? The location is defined in the X10 section...at least where it is on a Linux system...but I missed that for a while. I guess the minimum competence level required is knowing that the Linux "/" is the Windows "\;" if you don't realize that, you're probably in for some tough sledding.

In my experience, software/system designers are the wrong people to be writing manuals; they assume a level of experience the vast majority of potential users don't possess. They also often use jargon very familiar to the mavens, but patently unfamiliar to newbies. For example; long-time Windows users are accustomed to installing programs in the "Program Files" directory, so that's where I installed OR. Heeding the warning to not use a name with spaces in it, I figured all would be well if I used the directory name "OpenRemote;" no spaces. After flailing for three hours, I noticed in one of Marcus Redeker's posts a screen capture that showed that he had installed it in the root directory; I moved it, and voila, it worked. Yes, the documentation was correct, "Program Files" has a space in it and won't work...stupid me. But if the documentation simply recommended installing it in the root directory, and included the caveat that the ENTIRE path name must contain no spaces, then the people that decide to deviate from the recommended installation would be the ones flailing.

I realize that since OR runs on so many platforms and can do so many things, that a simple "Getting Started" might be a challenge, since different users will be interested in their own subset of the system's capabilities. I could write a very simple "How To" section for people wanting to set up an X10 system on a Windows 7 computer, for example, but it would be worthless unless that was the exact application of interest.

Also, operating at the command prompt level in Windows might be beyond the typical potential user, but a simple cmd level tutorial could solve that. After screwing with the Raspberry Pi, and then opening the cmd window in Windows, I typed "ls" to see what files were present, and saw the "Invalid command" message. Oh yeah, "cd" is "cd," but "ls" is "dir."

As I mentioned at the outset of this admittedly lengthy post, I recommend developing a comprehensive Table of Contents that would simply be an organized set of links to the already existing documentation. Over the past few days, I can't count the times I've said to myself, "Now where did I see that described," and then had to perform a search to find it again. (Oh, I need an ax to kill the troll...I know I saw one, but which room was it in???)

And now, since I'm about to tackle the next challenge...a "rules" primer would also be nice. The cat bell needs to chime when the cat wants in, not when I press a button on the tablet.

To everyone who has been responsible for developing and supporting OR thus far...congratulations on creating such an amazing system!
