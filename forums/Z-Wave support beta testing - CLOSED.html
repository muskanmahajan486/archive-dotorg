Posted by martinw at Aug 23, 2012 18:54

DIM is not used for status but for the slider. If you have a dimmer you need the following commands:

  • on
  • off
  • dim
  • status (only once)

If you want a switch, you also need a switch sensor (using the status) and the on/off commands

If you want a slider, you need a range sensor (1-100) (also using the same status) and the din command.

I just tested it and slider is updated when switching or vice versa.

Martin,

Perhaps it wasn't the best choice of words and so remove "ironic" if that makes you feel better. I didn't mean it as a diss to you, au contraire. It is the fact that your thought process around "pain-reward" is totally straightforward and valid. The 'irony' is internally, the message was more targeted at OR than you.

For the little story, I just went through the beta-testing myself for z-wave as I will deploy it in my new home (with z-stick stick and leviton z-wave switches) and so went through all the steps you went through. Except with a little wrestling (and support from Marcus) I got it to work in 2 days but I struggled with the drivers (which were out of date on the z-stick website) and struggled with the UI provisioning (mapping all the sensors and actions and whatnot). I got it to work but also realized that a lot of steps could be automated. This is not a negative reflection on OR in my opinion and is to be expected from a beta testing program (the manual steps). Furthermore the modus operandi of open source is to iterate and only bring features to market when they make sense. But the next steps are fairly obvious at this stage and as Marcus indicated the generation of objects side of things is something he has already tackled in his box and I am waiting for that before automating the house where I will have on the order of 100 switches and I would rather have the OR designer generate the 10 objects it needs to represent a switch (amounting to 1000 steps in my case, so YES PLEASE). These are kinks that will be ironed out as "productization" follows raw development. I want to stress that this is also how open source is intended to work, where we actually decide on features based on use and feedback as opposed to us sitting in a room and 'dreaming the world'. OSS can be quite good at usability based on that feedback, but that usability is something we need to focus on (and are focusing on) as the market we are in is very different indeed from the linux market.

The irony, to put your mind at rest, is that we are talking a lot about marketing a box with z-wave by default and making the ease of use tools available in a more commercial way. This is what vera does. So the irony is that you provide a very clear datapoint on where ease of use needs to be as you decide to not pursue this venue. In a way I thank you for your help on that. So you have helped in your own way

OR is meant as a integration system in terms of what field protocols it uses. This is something I will put to use as I will have IR integrated as well to control a color LED setup in my basement. We think of OR as a abstract platform but making particular implementations (in this case z-wave) part of a default box, with narrower scope from the get go, would probably increase the adoption in the short term. While flexibility comes with complexity, the usability question is 'when', as a vendor we introduce complexity. Here we still leave too many details up front and lose you in the process, as opposed to a more "works out of the box" for narrow cases and letting the flexibility (with adjacent complexity) bit appear later in the process when you would have a working install and were looking to expand the system to other buses.

So your feedback is timely and relevant and speaks to some of the discussions that are going on internally within OR. It says "market a box with z-wave software stack integrated, potentially with a RF antena integrated, and make the generation tools available.". In other words "make a vera" which is something that is within reach of the OR team at this stage.

Thanks again for the feedback, you have played your part in the great ecosystem of OSS
